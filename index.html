As changing conditions reduce krill and enhance salps, the ocean's carbon cycle shifts with them.

Another positive climate feedback may have been spotted.

By Tate Hom

Published June 2, 2025 6:00 PM EDT

As warming waters push krill away and open the door for salps, the way the ocean processes carbon is shifting with them.

Deep in the Southern Ocean, some of the smallest animals on Earth might be changing the way our planet stores carbon. Antarctic krill, critical both to marine food webs and biogeochemical cycles, are declining in number and drifting further south in search of colder waters. In their absence, salps are moving in. They arenâ€™t as ecologically essential to predators, but might play an even bigger role in carbon export.

This isnâ€™t just a case of changing diets for penguins and whales. It could actually reshape how the ocean pulls carbon out of the atmosphere and sinks it to the deep sea. Researchers are now trying to put numbers to that shift. The main question is how the decline of krill and rise of salps is affecting vertical carbon sequestration.

To figure that out, scientists are combining two massive datasets. One is KRILLBASE, which tracks krill and salp populations across time and space. The other is the Southern Ocean Time Series, or SOTS, which collects data on carbon flux using sediment traps. By filtering both datasets to cover the same time periods and locations, the researchers can match population ratios to carbon export rates.

Once the data is cleaned and aligned, the statistical analysis begins. The first step is Pearson correlation to see if thereâ€™s any basic relationship between krill-to-salp ratios and the amount of carbon sinking out of the surface ocean. If a strong pattern shows up, theyâ€™ll move on to linear regression to try and quantify the trend.

If the link holds, the researchers can scale it up. Using the regression model, they estimate how much carbon export has been lost across the broader Southern Ocean. Those estimates then feed into larger climate simulations like the Global Carbon Budget, helping us understand how these species changes could be contributing to atmospheric COâ‚‚ buildup.

Some patterns may not follow a straight line. If the linear model misses important parts of the story, the team plans to test nonlinear models that might better capture the complexity of interactions between organisms and climate.

This entire effort relies on a combination of marine science, coding, and data analysis. With Python and other tools, the researchers are aiming to build the first quantitative estimates of how krill and salp transitions impact the global carbon cycle.

The team hopes to apply this method to other marine species swaps around the world. If similar patterns show up elsewhere, this might just be one example of a much broader phenomenon already underway across the oceans.
